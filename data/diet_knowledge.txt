== MACRONUTRIENTS ==

Carbohydrates:
Carbohydrates are the body's primary energy source. The WHO recommends that 45-65% of total daily calories come from carbohydrates. Complex carbs (whole grains, vegetables, legumes) are preferred over simple sugars. Avoid refined carbohydrates like white bread, pastries, and sugary drinks. Choose whole grain bread, oats, quinoa, and brown rice for sustained energy.

Protein:
Protein is essential for muscle repair, immune function, and enzyme production. The RDA is 0.8g per kg of body weight, roughly 50g per day for adults. High-protein foods include chicken, fish, eggs, dairy, legumes, tofu, and nuts. Adequate protein helps maintain satiety and preserves lean muscle mass. Aim for at least 10-35% of daily calories from protein.

Fat:
Dietary fat is crucial for brain health, hormone production, and fat-soluble vitamin absorption. Total fat should be 20-35% of daily calories (about 44-78g for a 2000 kcal diet). Focus on unsaturated fats from avocados, olive oil, nuts, and fatty fish. Limit saturated fat to under 10% of calories. Avoid trans fats entirely, as they increase LDL cholesterol and heart disease risk.

== SUGAR ==

Added Sugar:
The WHO strongly recommends limiting free sugars to less than 10% of total energy intake, ideally below 5%. For a 2000 kcal diet, this means less than 50g (preferably 25g) per day. High sugar consumption is linked to obesity, type 2 diabetes, dental cavities, and cardiovascular disease. Common hidden sugar sources: soda, fruit juices, flavored yogurt, cereals, sauces, and candy. Check labels for: high fructose corn syrup, sucrose, fructose, glucose, maltose, dextrose.

Natural vs Added Sugar:
Natural sugars in whole fruits and dairy come with fiber, vitamins, and minerals. They behave differently in the body compared to added sugars. A product with 10g sugar from fruit is far healthier than 10g from added cane sugar. Always check the "Added Sugars" line on nutrition labels.

== SODIUM ==

Sodium and Health:
Sodium (salt) is essential for fluid balance and nerve function, but excess intake raises blood pressure (hypertension) and increases stroke and heart disease risk. The WHO recommends less than 2000mg (2g) of sodium per day. Most people consume 3400mg+ per day. Major sodium sources: processed foods, restaurant meals, bread, canned goods, condiments, cheese. Low-sodium foods: fresh fruits, vegetables, unsalted nuts, plain meats.

Tips to Reduce Sodium:
Cook at home using herbs and spices instead of salt. Choose low-sodium or no-salt-added canned goods. Rinse canned beans and vegetables to remove extra sodium. Limit soy sauce, ketchup, and salad dressings. Read nutrition labels — look for less than 140mg per serving (FDA "low sodium" standard).

== FIBER ==

Dietary Fiber Benefits:
Fiber improves digestion, prevents constipation, lowers cholesterol, and reduces risk of type 2 diabetes and colon cancer. It also promotes satiety, helping control weight. The daily recommendation is 25-38g for adults (25g for women, 38g for men). Most people only get about 15g per day.

Types of Fiber:
Soluble fiber (oats, beans, apples, psyllium) dissolves in water and helps lower blood cholesterol and glucose levels. Insoluble fiber (wheat bran, vegetables, whole grains) adds bulk to stool and helps food move through the digestive tract. Aim for a variety of both types.

High-Fiber Foods:
Beans and lentils (15g per cup), split peas (16g per cup), artichokes (10g), avocado (10g per fruit), broccoli (5g per cup), oats (4g per half cup), chia seeds (10g per 2 tbsp), quinoa (5g per cup), berries (strawberries 3g, raspberries 8g per cup).

== CALORIES ==

Caloric Balance:
Calories measure the energy your food provides. The average adult needs 1800-2200 kcal per day, varying by age, sex, weight, and activity level. Eating more calories than you burn leads to weight gain; eating fewer causes weight loss. However, the source of calories matters — 200 calories from broccoli is dramatically different from 200 calories from soda.

Calorie Dense Foods:
High-calorie foods include nuts, oils, cheese, fried foods, pastries, and alcohol. Low-calorie, nutrient-dense foods include vegetables, fruits, lean proteins, and whole grains. Focus on nutrient density, not just calorie count.

== HEALTHY EATING PATTERNS ==

Mediterranean Diet:
The Mediterranean diet is one of the most studied and recommended dietary patterns. It emphasizes fruits, vegetables, whole grains, legumes, nuts, olive oil, and fish. It limits red meat, processed foods, and added sugars. Associated with reduced risk of heart disease, stroke, type 2 diabetes, and certain cancers.

DASH Diet:
The DASH (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension) diet is clinically proven to lower blood pressure. It emphasizes vegetables, fruits, whole grains, lean protein, and low-fat dairy. It limits sodium (1500-2300mg/day), red meat, sweets, and sugary beverages. Excellent for heart health and weight management.

Plant-Based Eating:
Diets rich in plants and lower in animal products are associated with lower risks of chronic disease. You don't need to be fully vegetarian — just increasing plant intake helps. Key nutrients to monitor on plant-based diets: B12, iron, zinc, calcium, omega-3, vitamin D.

Intermittent Fasting:
Intermittent fasting involves cycling between periods of eating and fasting. Common methods: 16:8 (fast 16 hours, eat in 8-hour window), 5:2 (eat normally 5 days, restrict calories 2 days). Can help with weight loss, insulin sensitivity, and metabolic health. Not recommended for pregnant women, children, or those with eating disorders.

== READING FOOD LABELS ==

Serving Size:
Always check the serving size first. Nutrient information is listed per serving, not per package. A bag of chips may show 150 calories but contain 3 servings — that's 450 calories if you eat the whole bag.

% Daily Value (%DV):
5% DV or less is considered low for a nutrient. 20% DV or more is considered high. Use this to determine if a food is high or low in specific nutrients. Aim for high %DV of fiber, vitamins, and minerals; low %DV of saturated fat, sodium, and added sugars.

Ingredient List:
Ingredients are listed in descending order by weight. If sugar, refined flour, or oil is in the first 3 ingredients, reconsider the product. Watch for multiple types of sugar listed separately (glucose syrup, high fructose corn syrup, cane sugar) — combined they may be the #1 ingredient. Shorter ingredient lists generally indicate less processed food.

== SPECIFIC DIET QUESTIONS ==

Is this product good for weight loss?
For weight loss, prioritize: high protein (keeps you full), high fiber (slows digestion), low added sugars, moderate healthy fats, and adequate but not excessive calories. Products with more than 500 calories per serving are likely inappropriate as snacks but may work as meal replacements with proper planning.

Is this product good for someone with diabetes?
For diabetes management, focus on: low glycemic index foods, limited added sugars, adequate fiber to slow glucose absorption, and controlled portions of all carbohydrates. Avoid products with more than 15g of sugar per serving. Look for whole grain sources rather than refined carbs.

Is this product good for heart health?
For heart health: limit saturated fat, avoid trans fats, reduce sodium (aim under 600mg per serving), choose products with fiber and omega-3 fatty acids. Products high in unsaturated fats from nuts, seeds, and fish are beneficial.

Is this product good for muscle building?
For muscle building: prioritize high protein (15g+ per serving), adequate calories to support training, and sufficient carbohydrates for energy. Limit excessive sugar and processed additives. Whole food sources of protein are preferred over heavily processed protein bars.

What should I eat for more energy?
For sustained energy: complex carbohydrates (oats, quinoa, sweet potato), adequate protein, iron-rich foods (lean meat, spinach, lentils), B vitamins (whole grains, eggs), and staying hydrated. Avoid sugar crashes from simple carbs — they give quick energy but lead to fatigue.

== VITAMINS AND MINERALS ==

Key Vitamins:
Vitamin A (vision, immune function): carrots, sweet potatoes, leafy greens. Vitamin C (immune support, collagen): citrus, bell peppers, strawberries. Vitamin D (bone health, immunity): fatty fish, fortified foods, sunlight. Vitamin B12 (nerve function, red blood cells): meat, dairy, eggs, fortified plant foods. Folate (cell division, especially important in pregnancy): leafy greens, legumes, fortified grains.

Key Minerals:
Calcium (bones, muscle function): dairy, fortified plant milk, broccoli, almonds. Iron (oxygen transport): red meat, spinach, lentils, fortified cereals. Potassium (blood pressure, muscle function): bananas, potatoes, oranges, beans. Magnesium (energy production, muscle/nerve function): nuts, seeds, dark chocolate, whole grains. Zinc (immune function, wound healing): meat, shellfish, legumes, pumpkin seeds.

== HYDRATION ==

Water Intake:
Adequate hydration is essential for every body function. General guideline: 8 cups (2 liters) of water per day, more if exercising or in hot weather. Thirst is not a reliable indicator — drink consistently throughout the day. Signs of dehydration: dark urine, fatigue, headaches, poor concentration. Beverages like herbal tea and water-rich foods (cucumbers, watermelon) count toward daily intake. Avoid excessive caffeine and alcohol as they are diuretics.

== PROCESSED FOOD AND ADDITIVES ==

Ultra-Processed Foods:
Ultra-processed foods (UPF) are industrially formulated with multiple additives and little whole food content. Examples: packaged snacks, instant noodles, frozen meals, flavored drinks, fast food. High UPF consumption is strongly linked to obesity, heart disease, diabetes, and depression. The NOVA food classification system ranks foods 1-4 by degree of processing.

Common Additives:
Preservatives (sodium benzoate, BHA, BHT) extend shelf life but should be consumed in moderation. Artificial colors (Red 40, Yellow 5) have been linked to hyperactivity in children. MSG (monosodium glutamate) is generally safe for most people in moderate amounts. Emulsifiers (lecithin, carrageenan) help texture but some may affect gut microbiome. When in doubt, less is more — whole foods are always preferable.
